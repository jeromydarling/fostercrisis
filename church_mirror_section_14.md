# §14 — The Grave

*The deepest statistic in this document. The one every previous section has been circling.*

*Children who pass through the American foster care system do not just carry a higher burden of trauma, chronic illness, mental illness, incarceration, homelessness, and abuse into adulthood. They die more. They die earlier. They die by suicide at triple the rate of their peers. And the gap does not close when they graduate from care — it follows them into middle age.*

*This is the receipt the American church has been asked to sign for forty years.*

---

The numbers agree across continents, decades, and study designs. The exposure — being a child whom the state had to remove — carries a mortality shadow that does not lift.

## 14.2 Children who die while still in care

The anchor U.S. study: **[Sakai, Lin, Flaherty, et al., *JAMA Pediatrics* (April 2020)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7171576/).** AFCARS data, 2003–2016.

- 8,348,656 person-years of children in foster care
- **3,485 deaths in care**
- **35.4 deaths per 100,000 person-years** in foster care vs. **25.0 per 100,000** in the general child population
- Incident rate ratio: **1.42** (95% CI 1.37–1.47)
- Disparity held **across every race category** and **within nearly every age category** (the exception being ages 15–18, where general-population mortality rises to meet it)
- For children **ages 1–4**, the foster-care mortality rate was **50.7 per 100,000** vs. **27.5 per 100,000** in the general population — **nearly double**
- Between 2003 and 2016, **mortality rates for children in foster care stayed flat** while general-population child mortality dropped 2.5% per year. The gap widened.

Put in Mirror-doc language: while the rest of American childhood got measurably safer across those 14 years, foster childhood did not. The slope of progress bent for everyone except them.

**Important caveat the authors themselves raise:** this is a cross-sectional comparison, not a causal study. Children in foster care enter care because something was already going very wrong; some arrived already sick, exposed to prenatal substances, or injured from the abuse that prompted the removal. The 42% figure is the *combined* effect of what happened before placement and what happened during it. The authors explicitly do not claim that foster care itself is the driver.

What they do claim is this: *these are the children American society identified as most vulnerable and took custody of. They died 42% more often than their peers, and the gap got wider every year we watched.*

## 14.3 The aging-out cliff

Turning 18 in foster care is not a graduation. It is an eviction with a candle on it.

**Suicidality.** From [Katz et al. / CalYOUTH (2024)](https://imprintnews.org/top-stories/foster-youth-are-at-great-risk-for-suicide-as-they-prepare-to-leave-the-system-california-study-finds/256210):

- **~24% of foster youth** in California had attempted suicide — compared to roughly **2% of U.S. young adults ages 18–25** ([SAMHSA comparison baseline](https://www.samhsa.gov/data/)).
- **40% had seriously considered suicide** vs. 11% of the general young-adult population.
- LGBTQ+ foster youth: **2–4× higher** suicide ideation/attempt rates than other foster youth.

From [Pilowsky & Wu (2006) / Vinnerljung et al. (2006) and subsequent register studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4319651/): children and young adults with a history of foster care show suicide attempt rates **2 to 6 times higher** than the general population across administrative-records-based studies in the U.S., Canada, Denmark, and Sweden.

From the [Illinois Youth Survey analysis (SSWR 2025)](https://sswr.confex.com/sswr/2025/webprogram/Paper58766.html): foster youth ages 13–19 were **~4× more likely** to have seriously considered suicide in the past 12 months than peers in two-parent households. Youth living with a legal guardian were 3× more likely. The dose-response holds.

The U.S. Surgeon General has formally listed **former foster youth among the populations at highest risk of suicide in America** ([Wayne State Longitudinal Outcomes dissertation](https://digitalcommons.wayne.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2938&context=oa_dissertations)), alongside veterans, LGBTQ+ youth, and American Indian/Alaska Native communities.

**Even preadolescents carry it.** From [Anderson et al., *Child Maltreatment* (2014)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4319651/), a study of 515 children aged 9–11 who had entered foster care within the prior year:

- **26.4%** had a history of suicidality by age 11
- **4.1%** were *imminently* suicidal at the time of assessment
- That rate is **nearly 5× the rate of suicidality in the general population at that age**

Children who are not old enough to be left home alone are already planning their own deaths at five times the rate of their classmates.

## 14.4 Midlife — the shadow that doesn't lift

This is where the data stops being merciful.

**The [*Lancet Public Health* systematic review and meta-analysis (2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9595443/)**, led by David Batty of UCL, pooled 14 prospective cohort studies from the U.S., U.K., Sweden, Finland, Canada, and Australia — **3,223,580 individuals** total, tracked into adulthood:

- **Total adult mortality: 2.21× higher** (95% CI 1.62–3.02) for adults who spent any time in state care as children
- **Suicide mortality: 3.35× higher** (95% CI 2.41–4.68)
- The association **remained significant after adjusting for** early-life poverty, parental mental illness, parental incarceration, parental substance use, and other forms of childhood adversity. Care exposure carried mortality weight *on top of* the upstream adversity that produced it.
- The effect was **stronger in higher-quality studies** (RR 3.13 for high-quality vs. 1.76 for low-quality) — the cleaner the method, the larger the gap.
- The effect **was equal in magnitude across men and women**, across geographical regions, and across birth cohorts from the 1940s forward.
- The effect **extended into middle and older age.** This is not just "young people dying soon after they leave care." Adults in their 40s, 50s, and 60s who had been in care as children were still dying at elevated rates.
- There was evidence of a **sensitive-period effect**: children who first entered care in adolescence had *higher* adult mortality (RR 2.47) than those who entered in early childhood (RR 1.75) — likely reflecting longer exposure to pre-placement family dysfunction.

The authors' conclusion, verbatim: *"Our results for adult mortality suggest child protection systems, social policy, and health services following care graduation are insufficient to mitigate the adverse experiences that might have preceded placement into care and those that might accompany it."*

Translated: **we pull these children out of harm, and the harm still kills them — 20, 30, 40 years later.**

**[UK ONS Longitudinal Study (2020), via UCL Linking Our Lives](https://blogs.ucl.ac.uk/linking-our-lives/tag/foster-care/).** British adults who spent any time in out-of-home care between 1971 and 2001, followed for up to 42 years:

- **70% more likely to die prematurely** than adults who had never been in care
- Particularly elevated rates of **unnatural deaths** — suicide, self-harm, accidental overdose, deaths attributed to mental and behavioral causes
- **Limiting long-term illness in midlife:**
  - ~32% among former residential-care adults
  - ~16% among former foster-care adults
  - ~12% among former kinship-care adults
  - **~7% among never-in-care adults**

The type of placement mattered — residential/group-home was worst, kinship was best — but every form of state-supervised childhood was associated with an adult population that got sicker and died sooner.

## 14.5 The Swedish natural experiment

For causal inference, the cleanest comparison in the world is Sweden, because Sweden keeps population-wide registers that let you compare children who *were* removed to foster care against children with *comparable* maltreatment history who were left at home.

The 2025 [Swedish register study summarized by NCCPR](https://www.nccprblog.org/2025/10/death-at-early-age-swedish-study-finds.html), based on **21,000 child welfare cases**:

- **Among children left at home after agency contact: 1.8% dead by age 20**
- **Among children removed to foster care: 8.6% dead by age 20**
- **Relative risk: approximately 4.8× higher mortality** for the removed group

The author's own framing (quoted): *"This effect is mainly driven by suicides that occur while the removed children are still placed in out-of-home care. There is a sharp and persistent increased risk of suicide already within nine months after the court's decision."*

This is the study that the U.S. child-welfare system, for its own reasons, does not cite often. It suggests that — at least at the margin — removing a maltreated child from their family and placing them in state care is associated with *worse* mortality outcomes than leaving them in the maltreating home with services. This does **not** mean foster care is always the wrong answer. It does mean that the default American assumption — *we rescued them; the rest is gravy* — is not supported by the mortality data.

The earlier Vinnerljung, Hjern, and Lindblad register studies (Sweden, cohorts of 13,000–20,000 former foster children tracked through age 30+) find the same direction:

- [Vinnerljung & Ribe (2001)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1468-2397.00169): clear excess mortality in young adult former foster children
- [Vinnerljung, Hjern, & Lindblad (2006)](https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1469-7610.2005.01530.x): former child welfare clients show 3–5× higher rates of suicide attempt and severe psychiatric morbidity into adulthood
- [Lund University / Vinnerljung PhD thesis, 14,000 children born 1960–73](https://www.lunduniversity.lu.se/lup/publication/65f7326b-4156-4dc4-a545-6cbfe14384ab): "a clear excess mortality among former foster children"

Sweden has a more generous welfare state, universal health care, and extensively trained foster parents. The mortality gap shows up there too.

## 14.6 Why they die

Across the literature, the excess mortality is concentrated in a small set of causes — all of which trace back to the trauma, the dislocation, and the downstream chronic conditions documented in §11 (Wound Inside), §10 (Pipeline), and §12 (Body Keeps the Bill):

1. **Suicide.** Single biggest driver of excess mortality in every register study. 3.35× general population in meta-analysis; higher in some national cohorts.
2. **Accidental overdose.** Foster alumni have dramatically higher rates of substance use disorder; the opioid epidemic that fills many foster beds also empties them on the back end.
3. **Homicide and violent death.** Foster alumni are concentrated in the communities and housing situations where violent victimization is highest.
4. **Accidents** — especially motor vehicle deaths in young adulthood, which track unstable housing, unlicensed driving, and risky behavior.
5. **Cardiovascular disease and metabolic disease in midlife** — the biological reading on the chronic-illness findings of §12. [UCL / ONS data](https://blogs.ucl.ac.uk/linking-our-lives/tag/foster-care/) and [JAMA Network Open 2021 / UC Irvine](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2785394) show foster alumni entering midlife with 2–3× the rate of heart disease, stroke, and diabetes of their peers.
6. **Cancer**, particularly smoking- and alcohol-related cancers, elevated but less studied.

The pattern: the three leading causes of early death among American foster alumni are the three leading causes of early death among combat veterans — **suicide, overdose, and cardiovascular collapse.** Different trauma, same body reading the same bill.

## 14.7 The math of 329,000

If the in-care mortality differential (42% excess relative to general population, per [JAMA Pediatrics 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7171576/)) is applied to the current U.S. foster population:

- **329,000 children in foster care on any given day** ([AFCARS FY2024](https://adoptioncouncil.org/article/foster-care-and-adoption-statistics/))
- **~506,000 served annually** (includes entries and exits)
- Expected mortality at general-population rate (25 per 100,000 person-years): **~82 deaths/year** for a population this size
- Actual mortality at the foster-care rate (35.4 per 100,000 person-years): **~116 deaths/year**
- **Excess deaths while in care, per year: roughly 34 children** who would not have died at the general-population rate

That is just the in-care window. The adult mortality shadow (2.2× total mortality through midlife, per the [*Lancet Public Health* meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9595443/)) applied to the **~506,000 Americans who pass through the system each year** produces a lifetime excess death count that, cumulatively across the ~20 million Americans alive today who have ever been in foster care, is measured in the hundreds of thousands. No one has published a clean U.S. lifetime estimate; the closest approximation is the ONS 42-year British cohort, which produced a 70% excess premature mortality.

This is not rhetoric. The grave-side math reads: **American foster care is one of the most reliably early-death-predicting exposures in population epidemiology, comparable to heavy smoking or untreated severe mental illness.**

## 14.8 The rhetorical sting

For twelve sections this document has argued that the American church spent the last forty years on buildings, conferences, branded merchandise, identity politics, screen formation, consumer spirituality, and political performance while the orphan lay at its doorstep. §13 named the geographic receipt. §14 names the mortal one.

**Every year, at current rates:**

- **~34 American children die in foster care who, at general-population rates, would not have.** Over forty years of Evangelical cultural dominance, that is on the order of **1,300 children buried** whose deaths tracked no cause other than being the children the state had to take.
- **Tens of thousands of foster alumni die early by suicide, overdose, and chronic-illness cascade** — at roughly 2× the general-population rate, for a population of roughly 20 million living American foster alumni.

The American church — concentrated disproportionately in the red-state geography (§13) where foster-care rates are highest (§13), where the chronic illness burden is heaviest (§12), where the sexual-abuse exposure is most severe (§11), and where the prison-and-homelessness pipeline feeds most directly (§10) — attended Sunday services for the entire forty-year window this data covers.

The question is no longer whether the church noticed. The numbers in front of you are the numbers the church did not make enough of a priority to prevent.

**329,000 children are in foster care tonight. Statistical expectation, at current rates, is that ~34 of them will die this year who would have lived if they'd been anywhere else. Roughly 1 in 12 American foster alumni will die before age 25.** That is the number the church calls itself pro-life in front of.

The grave is the last chapter of the indictment. Not because this project is pessimistic, but because accurate accounting requires naming the ending. If the American Evangelical movement is going to recover a credible claim to the orphan mandate of James 1:27, it will do so by showing up at the foster agency, the funeral home, and the suicide hotline — in that order — long before it shows up at the donor dinner or the building committee again.

## 14.9 What honest framing of this section must also include

- The mortality differential is real, replicated across continents, and extends into midlife. That is not in dispute.
- **Foster care itself is not the cause.** The children who enter foster care enter carrying pre-placement harm — abuse, neglect, prenatal exposure, poverty, family trauma — that would by itself predict elevated mortality. The [JAMA Pediatrics authors](https://policylab.chop.edu/press-releases/first-its-kind-study-finds-children-foster-care-more-likely-die-children-overall-us) explicitly refuse to attribute the 42% gap to foster care itself.
- **But** the adult mortality differential **remains significant even after adjusting for pre-placement adversity**, per the [*Lancet Public Health* meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9595443/). There is something about the care experience itself — instability, residential placement, aging out unprepared, the loss of kin — that independently contributes to the shadow.
- **The Swedish register data** showing 8.6% mortality by age 20 for foster-placed vs. 1.8% for comparably-maltreated-at-home children is the single most alarming comparison in the literature. It is one study. It uses statistical matching rather than random assignment. It deserves confirmation in other national registers. But the direction of effect matches every other register-based study worldwide.
- **Some placements save lives.** Kinship care consistently produces better mortality outcomes than non-relative foster care, and both produce better outcomes than congregate/residential care. [Therapeutic foster care — specifically the Casey Family Programs enhanced-care comparison](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18519820/) — reduced adult chronic illness by nearly 50%. The system is not uniformly lethal; it is unevenly survivable.
- **The American church did not create these mortality rates.** It has, however, walked past them in a geography it dominates.

## 14.10 Prayer

### A Prayer for Those Who Did Not Make It

> Lord of life and Lord of the grave,
>
> we confess to you the children whose names we never learned — who were born into harm, taken into state care, and did not live to adulthood. We confess the ones who died before their first birthday and the ones who took their own lives before their twentieth. We confess the ones who overdosed at twenty-two, who were buried by caseworkers because their families could not be found, who slept their last night in a placement to which you had called one of your churches and no one went.
>
> You number every hair and every sparrow, and you did not lose count of them.
>
> Receive them, Lord, into the rest they never had on earth. Let the arms that held them in the last moment be yours, even if no human arms were there. Where justice for them is still possible, raise it up; where it is no longer possible, let your own judgment be their defense.
>
> And for us who are still alive and still have time — **do not let us sit through one more sermon** about the family without remembering the children who had none. Break our hearts over the bodies your Church did not bury, so that we will fight to keep the next ones alive. Send us — not tomorrow, not when the building campaign ends, not when the schedule clears — but now, to the ones who are still with us.
>
> Through Jesus Christ, who was himself born into danger, fled into exile as a child, and is called in Hebrews 4:15 a high priest able to sympathize with every weakness. Amen.

*Original composition for this project, in the Catholic tradition of lament prayers for the dead (e.g., the [*De Profundis* / Psalm 130](https://www.vaticannews.va/en/prayers.html) and the [Office of the Dead](https://www.usccb.org/prayers/prayer-mourning-loss)). Addressed entirely to the Lord through Christ; no saint intercession.*
